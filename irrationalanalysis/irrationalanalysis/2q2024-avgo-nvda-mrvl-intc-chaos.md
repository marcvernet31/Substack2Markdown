Canceling 20A is actually a really bad sign. Intel 18A is a derivative node of 20A, similar to how TSMC N4 and N4P are derivative nodes of N5.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-Vwl!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff6f7cded-7e17-426e-9133-8f67f0d74e05_1000x397.webp)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-Vwl!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff6f7cded-7e17-426e-9133-8f67f0d74e05_1000x397.webp)Source: [WikiChip](https://fuse.wikichip.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/wikichip_tsmc_logic_node_q2_2022-2.png)

Here is how derivative nodes work:

  1. Low single-digit lithography mask shrink.

  2. Updated (slightly better) standard cells and libraries.

  3. Minor improvements in expected [process skew.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_corners)

  4. A combination of all of the above.




TSMC N7 and N7P and N6 are not fundamentally different.

Same for TSMC N5, N5P, N4, and N4P.

The performance/power gains from derivative nodes are heavily dependent on the design, often not as good as advertised by the foundries. This is true for every foundry. 

**Intel’s claim that they are canceling 20A because “18A is so good” is nonsensical.**

A much more logical explication is 18A is not going well for 3rd party customers such as Broadcom and they are surging engineers to fix it.
