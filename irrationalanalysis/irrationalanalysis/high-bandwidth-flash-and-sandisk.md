My first thought is that Sandisk made zero mention of durability.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9tqM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F06d2af63-6efd-4365-8fa1-7bd0af6aac39_1686x930.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9tqM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F06d2af63-6efd-4365-8fa1-7bd0af6aac39_1686x930.png)

NAND flash has a write endurance. Controllers must track bad blocks and remove them from the mapping table. None of the slides presented by Sandisk indicate they have made architectural changes to the NAND cells to improve durability. 

Given how aggressively AI researchers complain about silent data corruption (SDC), this seems like a nightmare. NAND flash wear out is a well-known reality. Every commercial product has over-provisioning. Extra “hidden” capacity that is used to replace worn out cells over the expected life of the device.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BNmb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6d0d7f45-f809-44e7-a2c5-5d9e37529b2a_494x363.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BNmb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6d0d7f45-f809-44e7-a2c5-5d9e37529b2a_494x363.png)

There is also the issue of read/write granularity. 

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!o_FD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbbcbd4bd-e746-42fe-97de-e7f557a9a174_623x302.webp)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!o_FD!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbbcbd4bd-e746-42fe-97de-e7f557a9a174_623x302.webp)

NAND flash is designed to be read and written at a page level. 

Page size is usually 4-16 KB.

 **HBM3 is accessed in 32-byte packets.**

Bytes. Not kilobytes. 

I make have gotten the exact numbers wrong, but this is directionally correct.

 **Key Flaws in Sandisk High-Bandwidth Flash Concept:**

  1. NAND endurance will lead to horrible error rates and silent data corruption.

  2. Reads and writes are in far larger chunks (less granular) when compared to HBM/DRAM.

    1. Existing software is not written to handle this limitation.

    2. The changes needed on the controller side are not “minor”… they are massive!
